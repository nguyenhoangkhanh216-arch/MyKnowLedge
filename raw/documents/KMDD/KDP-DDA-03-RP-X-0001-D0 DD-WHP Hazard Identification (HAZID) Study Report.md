1. 
INTRODUCTION 
PVEP–KHANH MY (herein referred to as COMPANY) is undertaking development of 
Khanh My and Dam Doi fields, which are located in Block 46/13, Malay - Tho Chu Basin, 
S. R. Vietnam, approximately 205 km southwest of Cape Ca Mau and about 30 km northwest 
of the Bunga Kekwa field of Block PM3 CAA, with a water depth of approximately 53 m. 
The Project First Gas is expected in Quarter 4 of 2027. 
Location map of Block 46/13 is as shown in Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1 Location map of Block 46/13 
The development of Khanh My and Dam Doi fields will comprise of the following new 
facilities: 
- 
One (1) unmanned wellhead platform KM-WHP. 
- 
One (1) unmanned wellhead platform DD-WHP. 
- 
20” x 6.7 km long FWS rigid pipeline from KM-WHP to DD-WHP. 
- 
20” x 22.0 km long FWS rigid pipeline from DD-WHP to the existing BK-C WHRP.

3.2 OVERVIEW 
HAZID is an early identification and assessment of the HSE hazards, and it provides essential 
input for project development decisions. 
The HAZID technique is: 
- 
A way to identify and describe credible major accident hazards, occupational hazards, 
and other threats at the earliest practicable stage of a project or venture. 
- 
A fast-screening process focused on identifying and describing potential issues, not on 
solving them. 
The HAZID Workshop methodology consists of: 
- 
IDENTIFY:  
First, the study boundary was defined and the platform was divided into specific areas 
or decks for detailed assessment. Once the boundary is set, the session begin with a 
brief introduction by Operations or the Designer, explaining the intended design and 
operation of the process and utility systems, as well as the installation in general. 
- 
ASSESS: 
The team then brainstorms relevant threats using guidewords to identify what could 
cause the hazard. The possible worst-case consequences were discussed and 
documented. This includes reviewing historical incident data, e.g. from the industry, 
within the COMPANY organization, or from the current field location. Hazards with 
catastrophic consequences (Severity 5) shall be classified Major Accident Hazards 
(MAH) and aligned with the examples provided in the HSE & Fire Protection Design 
Basis & Philosophy for DD-WHP & KM-WHP (KDP-OOO-02-PH-X-0001). 
Existing safeguards for both prevention and mitigation of the hazard were identified 
and recorded using the HAZID worksheet (APPENDIX B).

3.3 HAZID ASSUMPTIONS 
Throughout the HAZID study workshop, the following assumptions was adopted: 
- 
In principle, only a single failure leads to a hazard – no double jeopardy, unless clearly 
credible. 
- 
All equipment is well designed, properly manufactured, and adequately inspected. 
- 
The facilities are well maintained and operated to an acceptable standard. 
- 
The system performs as intended by the design. 
- 
Failures of safeguarding or interlock systems are not considered.

5.2 MAJOR ACCIDENT HAZARD 
Major Accident Hazard (MAH), as defined by the UK Health and Safety Executive (HSE) 
Offshore Safety Case Regulations, are critical incidents that stem from unplanned or 
uncontrolled events during offshore oil and gas activities. These incidents have the potential 
to cause catastrophic outcomes, including multiple fatalities, severe personnel injuries, 
extensive damage to offshore infrastructure, and serious environmental consequences, 
particularly to the marine ecosystem in which these installations operate.  
Those hazards classified as “High” and “Very High” or rated with Severity Level 5 based on 
the  PVEP-KHANH MY Risk Assessment Matrix (RAM) (Doc. No.: KM-H-07-OOO-01), 
such as leading to multiple fatalities, major environmental impact, major asset damage and/or 
major reputation impact, shall be considered as MAH.  
Example of MAHs included, but no limited to: 
 Fires; 
 Explosions;  
 Loss of well control (such as blowout); 
 Structural failure of the platform; 
 Collision with vessels; and  
 Dropped object.
