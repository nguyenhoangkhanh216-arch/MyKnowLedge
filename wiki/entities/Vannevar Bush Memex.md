## Summary

The Memex was a theoretical personal knowledge device described by Vannevar Bush in his 1945 essay "As We May Think." It envisioned a desk-sized machine that could store all of a person's books, records, and communications, and allowed rapid retrieval via "associative trails" — links between documents that could be saved and shared. It is the spiritual predecessor of hypertext, the web, and modern personal knowledge management.

## Key Points

- **Year:** 1945, described in the Atlantic Monthly essay "As We May Think."
- **Core vision:** Private, curated knowledge store where the *connections between documents* are as valuable as the documents themselves.
- **Associative trails:** Named sequences of linked documents representing a line of thought — saveable and shareable.
- **Unsolved problem:** Bush's vision required a human to maintain the links and associations — the maintenance burden was never solved.
- **LLM resolution:** The LLM Wiki pattern is the closest practical realization of the Memex: private, curated, associative — with the LLM handling the maintenance Bush couldn't automate.
- **Contrast with the web:** The public web became the dominant hypertext system, but it diverged from Bush's vision — it's public, not private; crawled, not curated; indexed by machines, not maintained by humans.
